How Amazon Ads Work & What You'll Actually Pay

On our call there was some understandable confusion about what you've paid for, so let's clear it up first. There are two completely separate costs, and only one of them is "PPC."

What it isWho you payWhat it covers
Management fee (already paid)AMZ One StepOur work — building, launching, and optimizing your ad campaigns, keyword research, reporting, and the creative on your listing. A flat service you've already covered.
Ad spend / "PPC" (not yet started)Amazon, directlyThe money that actually buys the ad clicks. This is not included in what you paid us — Amazon charges it separately. This is the $1,000–$1,500/month we discussed.

PPC means "pay-per-click." You only pay when a shopper actually clicks your ad — not when it's shown. In our category a click currently costs around $0.75 on average, and up to $1.00–$1.25 for the most competitive terms like "rocks for painting."

How Amazon collects the ad money

Amazon deducts your ad spend from your sales first. If in a given period your ad spend is larger than your sales (very common in the first weeks of a launch, when sales are still small), Amazon charges the difference to your card on file. So early on, expect some charges to hit your card directly; as sales grow, more of it comes out of sales instead.

2.1Two numbers we'll manage the account by

  • ACOS (Advertising Cost of Sale) = ad spend ÷ sales from ads. If you spend $30 on ads and get $100 of ad-driven sales, ACOS is 30%. Lower is better. It tells us how efficient the ads themselves are.
  • TACOS (Total ACOS) = ad spend ÷ total sales (ads + organic). This is the number that decides whether the whole business makes money. As organic (non-ad) sales grow, TACOS falls — and that's the real goal of a launch.

2.2The kinds of ads we'll run (plain English)

  • Sponsored Products — the ads that look like normal search results. This is 80–90% of what we'll run.
  • Sponsored Brands (video) — a short video ad at the top of search. We add this once you have a few reviews; it suits a story-driven brand like yours.

Within Sponsored Products, we choose how tightly Amazon matches a shopper's search to your ad — these are called match types:

  • Auto — Amazon decides what searches to show you on. Cheap way to discover which terms convert.
  • Broad — matches related searches (e.g. "rock painting gift"). Good for discovery.
  • Exact — you pick the precise phrase (e.g. "rock painting kit"). Used to scale the terms we've proven make sales.
  • Product targeting — your ad appears directly on a competitor's product page.

The launch method is simple: use Auto and Broad to find what works cheaply, then move the winners into Exact and spend confidently there. Everything in Section 8 follows that logic.

4.4What thin margins mean for ads

A break-even ACOS of 8–11% means any ad that costs more than 8–11% of the sale loses money on that unit. For context, a launch normally runs 60–130% ACOS in the first months. So early ad sales are sold at a loss on purpose — we're buying search rank and reviews, betting that the organic (free) sales that follow will carry the profit later. That bet only pays off if we also fix the margin (Section 5). This is the crux of the whole plan.

Pricing, Discounts & Positioning

This is where the biggest decisions are. Pricing moves your break-even ACOS more than anything we can do inside the ad account.

5.14-Pack — raise to $24.99, with eyes open

The honest caveat

A price increase does not fix the 4-rock problem — it slightly widens the value gap versus 15–20 rock packs. Raising the price only works if the listing is repositioned hard as a premium keepsake/gift (large 4" smooth white rocks, size-comparison photos, gift framing) rather than a craft-supply value buy. Even then, expect the 4-pack to convert modestly. The genuine long-term fix is a higher-count SKU — e.g. a 10–12 rock pack priced to compete — but that's a sourcing decision for you, not a PPC lever. Flagging it because it matters more than anything in the ad account.

5.2Kit — hold $34.99 now, test $39.99 later, and push on cost

The Kit sits in a proven price band and has few true kit rivals, so I'd hold $34.99 through launch rather than discount it. But the $3.00 margin is the constraint, and there are two real levers:

  • Supplier cost (biggest lever). $17.52 landed is high for the contents. Even a $2 reduction takes gross profit from $3.00 to $5.00 (8.6% → 14.3% margin) and makes the whole launch materially easier. Worth a direct conversation with your supplier — this is the highest-leverage thing on the entire list.
  • Price test at $39.99 after ~30–50 reviews. Once social proof supports it, a higher price roughly doubles per-unit profit. We'd test it, not assume it, and watch conversion.

5.3Coupons — useful on the 4-pack, dangerous on the Kit

A coupon shows a green "Save X%" badge that lifts click-through, and you only pay when it's used (plus a small ~$0.60 redemption fee). But at these margins a coupon can quietly erase your profit. The real math:

The uncomfortable but important truth

Notice that even a good mature ACOS of ~30–45% is still above your break-even ACOS (9–26%). That means the ads themselves will not directly turn a profit at these margins — not in month 3, and honestly not even at scale unless price rises or cost drops. In plain terms: the account becomes profitable only when your TACOS drops below your gross margin % — driven by free organic orders, not by ads getting cheaper. Fixing the Kit's cost (Section 5.2) is what makes that reachable.

Amazon Vine — Your Reviews Engine

What it is: Amazon Vine is Amazon's official program that sends your product free to trusted reviewers ("Vine Voices") in exchange for honest reviews carrying a badge shoppers trust. It's the fastest legitimate way to go from zero reviews to a real review count.

Why it matters here: your competitors have 300–11,000 reviews; your 4-packs have none and the Kit only a handful. Even perfect ads convert poorly when a shopper sees "0 reviews" next to a $25–35 price. Reviews aren't optional for this launch — they're the thing that makes every ad click worth more.

Is This Launchable? & What to Decide on the Call

Yes — it's launchable and can generate real revenue. Whether it generates real profit depends on the margin decisions, not the ads.

What's genuinely working in your favor

  • A real, healthy market ($1.69M/month across the top products) that no single brand owns.
  • The Kit occupies a genuine gap — few premium adult kits exist, and it has a clean, affordable keyword base.
  • Low competition on dozens of specific long-tail terms you can win early.
  • An authentic US, artist-founded story in a category full of generic listings.
  • A temporary Amazon new-seller fee credit that doubles margin during the exact window we most need it.

What makes it hard (no sugarcoating)

  • Margins are thin — break-even ACOS of 8–11% means ads lose money on every launch sale, and even mature ACOS stays above break-even. This is the defining challenge.
  • The 4-pack's 4-rock count is a structural disadvantage that pricing alone can't fully solve.
  • Zero reviews at start — Vine helps but the first 60 days will be slow and expensive.
  • Profit is a months-6-to-12 outcome, and only if the margin levers are pulled.

Decisions for the July 1 call

  1. 4-Pack pricing: raise to $24.99 and reposition as a premium keepsake/gift? (Recommended — it's the only way the 4-pack is advertisable.)
  2. Amazon Vine: enroll both products (~$1,143, or less with fewer Kit units)? (Recommended — reviews are non-negotiable for this launch.)
  3. Coupons: 10% on the 4-pack, none on the Kit? (Recommended — a Kit coupon erases its margin.)
  4. Kit supplier cost: will you open a conversation about reducing the $17.52 landed cost? (Highest-leverage item on this list.)
  5. Inventory: confirm the 4-packs are actually sellable (they show 0 available) and that stock won't run out mid-launch.
  6. Budget commitment: hold ~$1,250/month for the full 90 days without pulling back after month 1 — launches that get defunded early always fail — and go in understanding month 1–3 runs at a loss by design.

Everything else — campaign builds, bids, negatives, weekly optimization and reporting — is on our side. My job on the call is to make sure you're comfortable with the trade-offs above before a dollar is spent.
