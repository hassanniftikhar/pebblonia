How Amazon Ads Work & What You'll Actually Pay

On our call there was some understandable confusion about what you've paid for, so let's clear it up first. There are two completely separate costs, and only one of them is "PPC."

What it isWho you payWhat it covers
Management fee (already paid)AMZ One StepOur work — building, launching, and optimizing your campaigns, keyword research, reporting, and the listing creative. A flat service you've already covered.
Ad spend / "PPC" (not yet started)Amazon, directlyThe money that actually buys the ad clicks. Not included in what you paid us — Amazon charges it separately. This is the $1,000–$1,500/month.

PPC means "pay-per-click" — you only pay when a shopper actually clicks your ad, not when it's shown. In the kit market a click currently runs about $0.91 on average (and ~$0.75 in the plain-rocks market), up to $1.00–$1.25 on the most competitive terms.

How Amazon collects the ad money

Amazon deducts ad spend from your sales first. If in a given period your spend is larger than your sales — very common in the first weeks of a launch — Amazon charges the difference to your card on file. So expect early charges to hit your card directly; as sales grow, more of it comes out of sales instead.

2.1Two numbers we manage the account by

  • ACOS = ad spend ÷ sales from ads. Spend $30, get $100 of ad sales → 30% ACOS. It measures how efficient the ads are.
  • TACOS = ad spend ÷ total sales (ads + organic). This is the number that decides whether the business makes money. As free organic sales grow, TACOS falls — that's the real goal of a launch.

2.2The ads and match types (plain English)

  • Sponsored Products — ads that look like normal search results. 80–90% of what we run.
  • Sponsored Brands (video) — a short video at the top of search; added once you have reviews.
  • Match types within Sponsored Products: Auto (Amazon picks the searches — cheap discovery), Broad (related searches — discovery), Exact (your precise phrase — used to scale proven winners), and Product targeting (your ad on a competitor's page).

The method: use Auto/Broad to find what converts cheaply, then move winners into Exact and spend confidently. Section 8 follows exactly that.

The Two Markets You're Entering

Your products don't compete in one market — they compete in two very different ones. All figures below come from live Helium 10 pulls dated July 1, 2026: the top competitors on "rocks for painting" (for the 4-pack) and on "rock painting kit" (for the Kit), plus their keyword exports.

4.2What thin margins mean for ads

A break-even ACOS of 8–11% means any ad costing more than 8–11% of the sale loses money on that unit. Launches routinely run 60–130% ACOS at first. So early ad sales are sold at a loss on purpose — we're buying rank and reviews, betting the free organic sales that follow carry the profit later. That bet only pays off if the margin also improves (next section). This is the crux of the whole plan.

Pricing, Discounts & Positioning

5.14-Pack — raise to $24.99, eyes open

At $24.99 the margin finally becomes advertisable: Heart earns $6.39 (25.6% margin), Round $5.98 (23.9%), lifting break-even ACOS to ~24–26% and moving it out of the weak $15–20 band.

The honest caveat

A higher price does not fix the 4-rock problem — it slightly widens the value gap versus 15–24 rock packs. It only works if the listing is repositioned hard as a premium keepsake/gift (large 4" smooth white rocks, size-comparison photos, gift framing). Even then, expect modest conversion. The genuine long-term fix is a higher-count SKU (a 10–12 rock pack) — a sourcing decision, not a PPC lever, but it matters more than anything we can do in ads.

5.2Kit — the pricing trap, stated plainly

This is the most important pricing reality in the whole plan. The premium mandala niche sells at $21.99–$24.99. You'd love to compete there. But your cost won't allow it:

The trap

Matching the market price means losing money on every Kit before you even pay for an ad. So the Kit is stuck: hold $34.99 (uncompetitive but profitable) or drop toward $25 (competitive but unprofitable). There is no clean answer at a $17.52 cost — which is exactly why cutting that cost is the highest-leverage move you can make. A drop to ~$13 landed would let you price at $27–29 and keep margin, transforming the launch.

Recommendation for launch

Hold $34.99 (you can't profitably go lower) and win on obvious value: a main image and A+ content that make the turntable, metallic paints, and ~40 pieces impossible to miss next to a $24.99 "dotting tools + paint" kit. In parallel, open the supplier conversation on cost now — it's what unlocks a competitive price later. Test $39.99 only much later, once reviews and rank are established.

The truth these numbers hide

Even a good mature ACOS of ~30–45% is still above break-even (9–26%). At these margins the ads themselves never turn a direct profit — not in month 3, not at scale — unless price rises or cost drops. Profit comes almost entirely from organic sales once you've earned rank. In plain terms: the account is profitable only when TACOS falls below your gross margin %, driven by free organic orders. Cutting the Kit's cost (Section 5.2) is what makes that reachable rather than theoretical.

Amazon Vine — Your Reviews Engine

What it is: Amazon's official program that sends your product free to trusted reviewers ("Vine Voices") for honest, badged reviews. The fastest legitimate way from zero reviews to a real count.

Why it's especially worth it for the Kit: remember from Section 3.2 that your real rivals sit at just 41–668 reviews. That's a wall you can actually climb. Getting the Kit to 30–60 credible reviews puts it in the same conversation as MoonGarden — and reviews are what let a $34.99 price convert at all.

8.6Organic growth & price testing (what you asked about)

  • Organic rank is the real prize — the free sales that make this profitable. Earned by ad-driven velocity + reviews + a strong listing. Ads and organic aren't separate projects; ads buy the early velocity that lifts organic.
  • Price testing: hold launch prices through the review-building phase; then test the 4-pack's premium framing and — only once reviews support it — a Kit move. If COGS drops, test a lower Kit price ($27–29) to attack the market directly; if not, a $39.99 test comes much later.
  • Listing quality is non-negotiable before scaling — the "complete kit" value must be obvious in the main image and A+ content (Ksenia's team). Advertising a listing that doesn't sell the value just pays to lose clicks.

Is This Launchable? & What to Decide on the Call

Launchable — yes. Easy or quickly profitable — no. This is a grind that pays off only if the cost and listing decisions are made, not just the ad ones.

What's genuinely working in your favor

  • The premium mandala-kit sub-niche has low review walls (41–668) — it's actually enterable, unlike the cheap kids'-kit market.
  • Your Kit is genuinely more complete than the $24.99 rivals — a real, defensible difference if the listing sells it.
  • Affordable, on-target adult/dot-art keywords with low competition to build the Kit on.
  • A temporary Amazon fee credit that doubles margin during the launch window.

What makes it hard (no sugarcoating)

  • The Kit is priced 40% above its niche and can't drop without losing money — the $17.52 cost is the gate.
  • Thin margins everywhere — break-even ACOS 8–11% means ads lose money through launch and even mature ACOS stays above break-even.
  • The 4-pack is structurally out-counted (4 rocks vs 15–100) — pricing can't fix that.
  • Both markets are Chinese-dominated and being a US brand earns you nothing.
  • Profit is a months-6-to-12 outcome, contingent on the cost/listing levers.

Decisions for the July 1 call

  1. Kit cost (highest leverage): will you open a supplier conversation to get the $17.52 landed cost down toward ~$13? This is what unlocks a competitive Kit price and real profitability.
  2. Kit price for launch: hold $34.99 and win on obvious completeness (main image + A+ content)? (Recommended — you can't profitably go lower today.)
  3. 4-Pack price: raise to $24.99 and reposition as a premium keepsake/gift? (Recommended — the only way it's advertisable.)
  4. Amazon Vine: enroll both, prioritizing the Kit (~$1,143 total, less with fewer units)? (Recommended — reviews are the cheapest rank you'll buy.)
  5. Coupons: 10% on the 4-pack, none on the Kit? (Recommended — a Kit coupon erases its margin.)
  6. Budget commitment: hold ~$1,250/month for the full 90 days without pulling back after month 1 — and go in understanding month 1–3 runs at a loss by design.

Everything else — campaign builds, bids, negatives, weekly optimization and reporting — is on us. My job on the call is to make sure you're comfortable with these trade-offs before a dollar is spent, especially the Kit's cost-and-price reality, which is the thing that most determines whether this becomes a profitable business or a break-even one.
